Part 1 Introduction & Research Question

This study examines how street design (cartway width, speed, micro-infrastructure) and environmental features (tree canopy, elevation grade) correlate with automobile crash frequencies and severities across Philadelphia's street segments.

In our version, we expand the original methodology to focus on risk-exposure rates rather than simple counts, integrating state and local traffic volumes (AADT) to normalize crash occurrence, and layering demographic equity indicators (Heat Vulnerability Index, School Zones) to examine environmental justice.

Part 3 Elevation & Grade Sampling

Street grade (slope) is estimated using a Digital Elevation Model (DEM) raster. Points are sampled at 10-meter intervals along each street centerline, retrieving coordinates, extracting elevation values, and calculating the grade as:

Grade = (Elevation_Max - Elevation_Min) / Segment_Length

A smoothing algorithm (moving average) is applied to mitigate elevation spikes on bridges, ramps, and overpasses.

Part 4 Risk-Exposure Baselining

Analyzing simple crash counts penalizes long, heavily traveled segments. We normalized crash rates to compute a true Risk Index representing crash frequencies per million Daily Vehicle-Feet traveled:

Risk Index = (Crashes × 1,000,000) / (AADT × Segment Length)

AADT values are joined from DVRPC and PennDOT. For local roads lacking telemetry, we apply class-based volume fallbacks:

Road Class Classification Assumed Daily Volume (AADT)
Class 1 Expressway 50,000
Class 2 Major Arterial 15,000
Class 3 Minor Arterial 7,000
Class 4 Collector 3,000
Class 5 Local / Residential 1,000

Part 5 Right-of-Way & Equity

We spatially snap the City of Philadelphia's Bike Network to segment centerlines, classifying right-of-way configurations: Protected Lanes, Painted Lanes, or Sharrows. Intersections are classified into Signalized, Stop-Controlled, or Uncontrolled points.

Additionally, we Buffer School Facility coordinates (500ft) and map Census Block Groups to identify high Heat Vulnerability Index (HVI ≥ 4) areas to support equity-focused street safety audits.

Part 6 Negative Binomial Modeling

Because crash counts are overdispersed integers, we fit Negative Binomial regression models to isolate design impact. The third model includes interaction terms (Canopy × Cartway Width, Grade × Speed Limit):

Variable Coefficient (Model 3) z-statistic P > |z| Interpretation
Intercept -1.4281 -12.44 < 0.001 Significant baseline offset
Cartway Width (ft) 0.0245 8.12 < 0.001 Wider roads increase crash frequency
Tree Canopy (%) -0.4512 -4.88 < 0.001 Canopy exhibits protective correlation
Grade (%) -0.0890 -2.14 0.032 Slope reduces frequency at low speeds
Canopy × Cartway Width 0.0112 3.62 < 0.001 Canopy's protective effect weakens on wide roads

Part 7 Conclusions & Handoff

The findings show that street design changes are correlated with safety outcomes. Narrower streets combined with robust tree canopy correlate with the lowest overall risk profiles.

This analysis was compiled into clean GeoParquet files (philly_segments.parquet) and handed off to the browser application. Using DuckDB-WASM and MapLibre GL, we render these analytical findings in real-time, letting users click any street segment in Philadelphia to inspect its local variables and see live DuckDB queries execute directly in their browser console.
