Philadelphia Environmental Safety Analysis

A street-segment study of crash density, micro-infrastructure, and neighborhood demographics.

Original project by Ben Polinsky
V2 Diagnostic Engine by Toshon Jennings & Antigravity (Google DeepMind)

Part 1 Introduction & Research Question

Original analysis (Ben Polinsky): This study examines how street design (cartway width, speed, micro-infrastructure) and environmental features (tree canopy, elevation grade) correlate with automobile crash frequencies and severities across Philadelphia's street segments. The original analysis used raw crash counts and focused on the physical and environmental variables available through OpenDataPhilly.

V2 expansion (Toshon Jennings): Added length-normalized crash density rates, layered demographic equity indicators (Heat Vulnerability Index, School Zones), snapped bike network and intersection control data to segments, and added temporal slices (day/night, weather) and environmental overlays (streetlight density, sun-glare prediction). The V2 engine also integrates a client-side Grounded AI Safety Assistant that translates natural language questions into DuckDB-SQL queries executed directly in the browser.

Part 2 Curbs & Cartway Widths

Original analysis (Ben Polinsky): To compute road widths, Ben joined curb line geometry to the street centerlines from OpenDataPhilly. Perpendicular transects were drawn at regular 75-foot stations along each segment, projecting outward until intersecting the curb lines. Divided streets with median islands and edge cases around intersections were resolved using median distance calculations. The cartway-width calculation is one of the core spatial operations in the pipeline, documented in the Jupyter notebook with multiple rejected routes and extensive validation.

Part 3 Elevation & Grade Sampling

Original analysis (Ben Polinsky): Street grade (slope) is estimated using a Digital Elevation Model (DEM) raster from OpenDataPhilly. Points are sampled at 10-meter intervals along each street centerline and their elevation values extracted from the raster. Grade is calculated as:

Grade = (Elevation_Max - Elevation_Min) / Segment_Length

A smoothing algorithm (moving average) is applied to mitigate elevation spikes on bridges, ramps, and overpasses. This grade variable is used in the regression models in Part 6 as a predictor for crash frequency.

Part 4 Crash Density Baselining

Original approach (Ben Polinsky): Ben's analysis used raw crash counts per segment as the dependent variable in the regression models, which is the standard approach when traffic volume data is unavailable.

V2 revision (Toshon Jennings): Initial V2 work attempted to normalize crash counts by traffic volume using PennDOT AADT where available and road-class-based estimates as a fallback — computing a Risk Index of crashes per million vehicle-miles. This approach was later removed because Philadelphia lacks reliable AADT for most local streets: PennDOT's state_aadt only covers state-owned roads, and DVRPC's traffic-count service mixes 15-minute, bicycle, pedestrian, and classification counts. The class-based lookups described the road's functional type, not the specific segment, so labeling the result "AADT est." was misleading. The metric was replaced with Crash Density — crashes per 1,000 ft of segment — which uses only measured quantities (crash counts and segment length from the centerline GIS) and makes no traffic-volume claim.

Crash Density = (Crashes × 1,000) / Segment Length (ft)

Part 5 Right-of-Way & Equity

V2 addition (Toshon Jennings): These layers were added as part of the V2 diagnostic engine and were not in Ben's original analysis.

The City of Philadelphia's bike network is spatially snapped to segment centerlines (max 30ft distance), classifying right-of-way configurations: Protected Lanes, Painted Lanes, or Shared lanes (sharrows). Intersection control data is categorized into Signalized, Stop-Controlled, or Uncontrolled points and snapped to segment endpoints.

Demographic equity overlays were added using Census Block Group data: school facility locations buffered at 500ft to flag school zones, and CDC-aligned Heat Vulnerability Index (HVI) tracts at HVI ≥ 4 threshold to identify high climate-risk corridors.

Part 6 Negative Binomial Modeling

Original analysis (Ben Polinsky): Because crash counts are overdispersed integers, Ben fit Negative Binomial regression models to isolate the impact of design and environmental variables. The third model includes interaction terms (Canopy × Cartway Width, Grade × Speed Limit):

Variable Coefficient (Model 3) z-statistic P > |z| Interpretation
Intercept -1.4281 -12.44 < 0.001 Significant baseline offset
Cartway Width (ft) 0.0245 8.12 < 0.001 Wider roads increase crash frequency
Tree Canopy (%) -0.4512 -4.88 < 0.001 Canopy exhibits protective correlation
Grade (%) -0.0890 -2.14 0.032 Slope reduces frequency at low speeds
Canopy × Cartway Width 0.0112 3.62 < 0.001 Canopy's protective effect weakens on wide roads
Grade × Speed Limit 0.0035 2.95 0.003 Steep slopes on high-speed roads increase risk

Part 7 Conclusions & Handoff

Original findings (Ben Polinsky): The regression results show that street design changes are correlated with safety outcomes. Narrower streets combined with robust tree canopy correlate with the lowest overall risk profiles. These findings were compiled into the GeoParquet data files used by the browser application.

V2 deployment (Toshon Jennings): This analysis was handed off to a client-side browser application using DuckDB-WASM and MapLibre GL, rendering the analytical findings in real-time. Users can click any street segment in Philadelphia to inspect its local variables, filter by crash severity or environmental conditions, and use the Grounded AI Safety Assistant to ask natural-language questions about the data.
